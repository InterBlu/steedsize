The Autumn Rugging Dilemma: When to Start and How to Layer

Francesca at SteedSize 18 May 2025 7 min read Seasonal
← Back to blog

Autumn is, without question, the trickiest rugging season of the year. Not winter — when it's clearly cold and the decision is mostly about how much warmth rather than whether to rug at all. Autumn, when temperatures swing fifteen degrees between morning and afternoon, when your horse's coat is mid-transition, and when every yard seems to have a different opinion about what constitutes a "rug on" day.

Here's a practical guide to navigating it.

The early rugging trap — and why it matters

The instinct to reach for a rug when the first chill arrives is understandable. What's less understood is that rugging too early, before your horse's coat has had the chance to grow, can actually interfere with the coat development process. Horses respond to shortening daylight hours and falling temperatures by growing a winter coat. Apply warmth artificially too soon, and you interrupt that signal.

This doesn't mean leaving horses cold — it means being measured about when and how you start. For most unclipped horses in the UK, the transition from no rug to lightweight rug happens somewhere between mid-September and mid-October, depending on where you are in the country and what the weather is doing that particular year.

The golden rule

Your horse's comfort should lead the decision — not the calendar, not what others on the yard are doing, and not your own feeling of cold. What matters is whether your horse is warm enough, not whether you are.

Clipped vs unclipped: two very different autumns

The autumn rugging question looks entirely different depending on whether your horse is clipped. These really are separate conversations:

Clipped horses

Warmth from day one

Any horse that's been clipped has lost their primary insulation. From the moment the clippers come off, your rug is doing the work their coat was doing. A freshly clipped horse in autumn needs immediate, appropriate coverage — don't let the mild daytime temperatures fool you; it's the overnight low that counts.

Unclipped horses

Let the coat do its job first

Horses with their natural coat have significant built-in insulation. A healthy winter coat can trap air and maintain warmth in ways a rug can supplement but rarely replicate entirely. Watch and respond to what you see — don't pre-empt what hasn't happened yet.

Reading your horse in autumn

The best rugging decisions in autumn are observational ones. Rather than following a fixed temperature threshold, look for the signs your horse gives you:

Signs they're cold

Shivering is the obvious one, but it's actually relatively rare in horses with a good winter coat unless they're genuinely cold or unwell. More commonly: a tucked-up posture, huddling with other horses, reluctance to move away from shelter, or a dull coat that's lost its usual condition. Feeling cold to the touch behind the ears or on the loins is also meaningful — these areas should feel warm even outdoors.

Signs they're too warm

Sweating under the rug (particularly at the chest and neck), a horse that's restless or pawing when rugged, or a damp coat when you remove the rug in the morning. Over-rugging is a common and underappreciated problem — a horse that's too warm can't regulate the way an under-rugged horse might, and sustained overheating can affect hydration and behaviour.

The layering approach: why it works

Rather than buying a new rug for every weather scenario, the most flexible system uses a waterproof outer shell plus interchangeable liners or stable rugs underneath. This is cheaper long-term and gives you far more range.

Here's how a typical autumn layering system might work:

Conditions Turnout Stable
Mild autumn day (12°C+) 0g waterproof shell only No rug or light fleece
Cool but dry (7–12°C) 0g shell + 100g liner 100g stable rug
Cold nights (2–7°C) Waterproof turnout, 200g 200g stable rug
Cold snap / first frost 200g turnout + neck cover 200g + light fleece layer

These are starting points for an unclipped horse in average condition. A clipped horse will typically need one weight heavier across the board. A horse that runs cold, or a fine-skinned or elderly horse, may also need more than this suggests.

Managing day/night temperature swings

This is the core autumn challenge. A 14°C afternoon can drop to 4°C by midnight — a 10-degree swing that a single static rug can't perfectly accommodate. There's no perfect solution to this, but there are practical ones:

Evening check-in

If you're at the yard in the evening, you can adjust then — go up a weight or add a neck cover for the overnight period. Most horse owners do this already, but it's worth being deliberate about it rather than defaulting to whatever was on at lunchtime.

Use the overnight forecast, not the daytime one

The question to ask when rugging for turnout overnight is: what is the lowest temperature this horse will experience before I'm back in the morning? That number — the overnight low — should drive the decision. The forecast for 3am matters more than the one for 3pm.

Accept that you won't always get it exactly right

On some days, no single rug will be perfect across a 12-hour stretch. That's okay. A horse that's slightly too warm for two hours of mild afternoon sun is not suffering — they're just a bit toasty. Aim for the right call most of the time, and trust that healthy horses in good condition have a reasonable tolerance band either side of ideal.

A note on elderly and underweight horses

Older horses and those carrying less condition going into winter need more careful management in autumn than a healthy horse in full weight. Their ability to thermoregulate declines with age, and they may not show the obvious signs of cold that a younger horse would. If your horse is older or body condition score has dropped, err on the side of more warmth earlier in the season — and speak to your vet about their condition going into winter.

Your autumn rug checklist

Before the cold arrives
  • ✓ Check all rugs for damage — re-proofing waterproofs before autumn is easier than discovering leaks mid-storm.
  • ✓ Wash any rugs stored since spring — moulds can develop in storage and affect horses with skin sensitivities.
  • ✓ Check all fastenings — buckles, surcingles, and leg straps deteriorate over time and can fail unexpectedly.
  • ✓ Note your horse's measurement — if you're buying anything new this season, having it ready saves a trip back to the yard.
  • ✓ Know your overnight lows — in autumn especially, the forecast you check at 6pm is different from the one at 10pm. Use a weather app that gives hourly or overnight lows.

Autumn rugging is genuinely one of the harder aspects of horse care to get consistently right — because the variables keep moving.
