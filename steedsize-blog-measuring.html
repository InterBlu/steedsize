How to Measure Your Horse for a Rug — And What to Do With the Number

Francesca at SteedSize 18 May 2025 6 min read Fitting & Sizing
← Back to blog

Rug sizing seems like it should be simple. It isn't, quite. Most horse owners have at least one rug in the tack room that doesn't quite fit — too long, too tight across the shoulders, gaping at the chest. Often, this comes down to a measurement that was slightly off from the start.

Here's how to do it properly, and what to do with the number once you have it.

The measurement that matters

UK rug sizes are based on a single measurement: the length of your horse's body, taken in a straight line from the centre of the chest to the furthest point of the hindquarters — not the tail itself, but the point where the tail meets the body.

This is the only measurement used for sizing in the UK and Ireland. Get it right, and you have a reliable foundation. Get it wrong, and no amount of brand-switching will help.

How to take the measurement

1

Get the right tape

Use a flexible measuring tape — a dressmaker's tape works perfectly. A metal tape measure is not suitable; it won't follow the curve of the chest. Some equestrian retailers sell colour-coded rug tapes, but a standard dressmaker's tape is identical in function.

2

Stand your horse on level ground

Ideally square — four feet evenly placed, head held naturally forward. An uneven stance or a horse craning its neck can affect the reading by an inch or two.

3

Find your starting point

Place the end of the tape at the centre of the chest — the bony midpoint at the front, roughly where a rug's chest fastenings would sit. Hold it firmly in place.

4

Run the tape in a straight line

Keep the tape as straight as possible along the horse's side, following the natural line of the body to the point of the hindquarters (the tail dock, not the end of the tail). Read the measurement in inches.

5

Measure twice

It sounds obvious, but rug returns are expensive. Take the measurement twice from scratch — if both readings agree, you're good. If they differ, take a third and use the most common result.

Common mistake

Don't measure to the end of the tail. UK sizing goes to the tail dock — where the tail meets the body. Measuring to the tail tip will give you a reading several inches too large and result in a rug that drags and bunches.

How UK rug sizes work

What to do if you're between sizes

It happens. Your horse measures 67 inches — right between a 5'6" and a 5'9". The default advice is to size up, because a rug that's slightly too long can be adjusted at the leg straps and is far less likely to cause pressure points than one that's too short. A rug that pulls at the shoulders restricts movement, causes rubs, and can slip back over time.

That said, it's not always that simple. If your horse is particularly wide across the hindquarters or has a short, broad back, a standard size up may sit poorly. In these cases, it's worth measuring the width specifically and contacting the retailer before buying — many will advise on fit for specific body types.

Fit is more than length

Once you have the right length, there are a few other fit factors worth checking:

Shoulder freedom

The most important fit point for movement. When your horse walks forward, the rug should move with them — not pull against the withers or drag backward. A rug that's cut too straight across the shoulder will restrict the foreleg and cause rubs in the girth area. Look for rugs with a contoured shoulder cut if your horse is particularly well-muscled or broad across the chest.

Chest fit

You should be able to fit a hand's width between the chest strap and your horse's chest — not more, not less. Too tight and it restricts breathing; too loose and it can rotate, pulling the rug sideways.

Belly fit

Cross surcingles (the straps that cross under the belly) should be adjusted so you can fit a hand underneath comfortably. Leg straps should be passed through each other in a figure-of-eight before fastening to prevent them swinging independently.

Breed note

Thoroughbreds and warmbloods often have high withers, narrow shoulders, and a long back — a combination that makes standard sizing trickier. Native breeds and cobs are frequently wider than their length measurement suggests. If your horse has an unusual shape, a brand known for wider cuts (such as those designed specifically for cobs) may fit far better than adjusting the size alone.

One final tip

Keep your measurement written somewhere accessible — ideally inside the tack room door or in your phone notes. It's surprising how often this comes up unexpectedly: a rug sale, a hand-me-down from a friend, a retailer with stock to clear at short notice. Having the number ready saves you measuring in a muddy field on a dark evening with an uncooperative horse.
