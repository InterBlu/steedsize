What Rug Fill Weight Actually Means — And How to Choose the Right One

Francesca at SteedSize 18 May 2025 8 min read Rugging
← Back to blog

Walk into any tack shop or scroll through any equestrian retailer's website and you'll be confronted with rug fill weights: 0g, 100g, 200g, 300g. Maybe a 250g, if a brand is feeling adventurous. But what do those numbers actually mean — and does a 300g rug for one horse work the same as a 300g rug for another?

The short answer is no. The longer answer is what this post is about.

What the number means

The fill weight figure — expressed in grams — refers to the amount of polyester wadding (hollow fibre or similar) packed into one square metre of the rug. So a 200g rug contains 200 grams of filling per square metre of its surface area. More filling means more trapped air, which means more warmth.

It's roughly analogous to the tog rating on a duvet, if that helps — though the comparison isn't perfect, because horse rugs are designed to cover a moving, outdoor animal rather than a sleeping person.

Worth knowing

Fill weight doesn't account for the shell fabric, any quilting pattern, or how well the rug fits your horse. Two 200g rugs from different brands can perform quite differently in practice.

The general weight guide

Most UK equestrian brands follow a broadly similar framework. Here's how the weights tend to map to conditions — though treat this as a starting point, not a rulebook:

Fill weight Typical use Temperature guide
0g / Lightweight Waterproof turnout with no warmth; rain sheet or fly rug 10°C and above (clipped horses)
100g Light warmth — good for mild autumn days or a horse that runs warm Roughly 5–10°C
200g Mid-weight — a true workhorse; covers a lot of British weather Roughly 0–7°C
300g+ Heavyweight — serious cold, hard winters, clipped horses Below 0°C, or 0–5°C for clipped/thin-skinned horses

These temperature ranges are deliberately broad — because the ambient temperature is only one factor. Which leads us to the part most guides skip over.

Why the same rug can mean very different things for different horses

A 200g rug on a fully coated native cob living out year-round is a very different thing from a 200g rug on a freshly clipped Thoroughbred in a draughty stable. The rug is identical. The horses are not.

These are the variables that matter most:

1. Coat condition and clip

An unclipped horse in full winter coat has substantial natural insulation — some researchers estimate this can account for the equivalent of several degrees of warmth. A horse with a hunter clip, or fully clipped, has lost much or all of that, and needs the rug to compensate. If your horse is clipped, you'll typically need to go up at least one weight — sometimes two — compared to an unclipped horse in the same conditions.

2. Where they live: stable vs field

Stabled horses are out of the wind and rain, which dramatically changes their warmth requirements. A stabled horse might be perfectly comfortable in a lighter stable rug while their field-kept companion needs a heavy-duty turnout. If your horse moves between both, you may well need two rugs at different weights — or a detachable liner system.

3. Individual metabolism: the "runs warm / runs cold" factor

Some horses genuinely run warm — they're the ones sweating under a rug on a chilly day, still bright-eyed when their paddock companions are huddled together. Others seem to feel the cold intensely, shivering before anyone else would think twice. This isn't just perception; horses do vary in how efficiently they regulate their own body temperature, and breed plays a role (more on that below).

4. Breed and build

Native breeds — fell ponies, Connemaras, Highlands, Dartmoors — evolved to handle harsh conditions. They tend to need less intervention than warmbloods or horses with Thoroughbred breeding, which often have finer skin, less natural fat coverage, and less winter coat. A chunky cob in a 100g at 3°C might be perfectly happy. A lean Thoroughbred in the same setup might not be.

The common mistake

The most frequent rugging error isn't under-rugging — it's over-rugging. A horse that's too warm can't simply take a layer off. Signs include sweating under the rug, a damp neck when you remove it, and restlessness. Over-rugging a horse with a natural coat can also interfere with their coat's ability to do its job.

If in doubt, check under the rug at the shoulder — it should feel warm but dry, never hot or clammy.

Using fill weight alongside liners

Rather than buying multiple rugs at different weights, many horse owners build a layering system. A good waterproof shell turnout rug combined with a detachable neck cover and a separate stable liner (100g or 200g) gives you far more flexibility than owning three separate heavyweight rugs at a cost that doesn't bear thinking about.

The maths is additive: a 0g waterproof turnout + 100g stable liner underneath = roughly 100g effective warmth. Some liner systems are designed to attach inside rugs directly; others just layer. Check your rug's compatibility before buying.

So what should you actually do?

Start with the temperature — but then layer in what you know about your horse. A horse that runs cold, is clipped, or lives out full time will almost always need more than the temperature alone suggests. A chunky unclipped native in a shelter-rich field will need considerably less.

When in doubt, check in the morning rather than at night. Overnight temperatures are what drive most rug decisions, but it's the morning check — before you've changed anything — that tells you whether the call was right.
